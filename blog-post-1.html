The Paradox of Meetings: Satirical Writing on Mental Olympics

Anu Surya June 2025 4 min read

The following piece is based on firsthand observation in the corporate trenches. It was born during a real-life meeting where a sudden spike in situational awareness forced me to confront the systematic flaws of our daily routines. Somewhere between the third alignment sync and the fourth revision of a roadmap timeline, I switched into observer detachment mode. I stopped taking notes and just started watching the room — mentally laughing as I realized I wasn't trapped in a project crisis. I was witnessing a high-stakes, professional sport.

"I realized this is: The Art of Doing Nothing Efficiently."

In modern organizations, productivity has reached an impressive milestone. We can now take 6 months to decide what we already agreed on in the first meeting. It is a marvel of human coordination.

The process begins with a simple idea - usually something dangerous like "let's improve efficiency" or "let's ship this faster." Immediately, the system activates its natural defense mechanism: the meeting calendar.

The Alignment Spiral

First comes alignment meeting 1: where we agree we need alignment. Then alignment meeting 2: where we refine the alignment about alignment. By meeting 7, we are dangerously close to execution — so we quickly schedule a workshop to slow things down.

Welcome to the Corporate Mental Olympics

Here, the core events require zero physical stamina but maximum bureaucratic agility. Teams compete fiercely in Synchronized Nodding while actively zoning out. The Responsibility Relay — the art of passing a hot-potato action item to another department before the call ends — is executed with breathtaking precision. No medals are awarded, but survival is its own reward.

At no point is the actual work done. That would be premature.

The Cult of Slides

Instead, we invest in something far more valuable: slides. Beautiful, carefully crafted slides that explain why work is important, how work might happen, and what work could look like if we ever decided to do it. Execution, of course, is a sensitive topic. It must not be rushed — unlike planning, which is best done repeatedly.

The Inclusive Genius

The true genius of this system lies in its inclusivity. Everyone has a voice. Every opinion is valued. Every stakeholder is invited. Even stakeholders who have not read the document are encouraged to comment on it.

By the time final approval arrives, the original idea has evolved into something safer, duller, and significantly more "aligned." Efficiency may have been lost somewhere along the way, but we have gained something priceless: consensus.

"And if, by chance, someone asks why nothing has been delivered yet, we can confidently respond: 'We are in the final phase of alignment.'"
